If you've ever stood in the sports drink aisle at Target and turned over a bottle of "kids' electrolytes" only to find Red 40, sucralose, and 21 grams of sugar staring back — same.

I have two kids under ten. One plays soccer. The other plays *whatever her brother is doing*, plus a 6:45am gymnastics class that I personally don't believe should be legal. Both of them sweat. Both of them complain about being thirsty in ways that always feel slightly suspicious. And for years, my answer was the same one most parents land on: a Capri Sun in the lunchbox, a Gatorade after the game, a Pedialyte when someone was throwing up.

Then I actually started reading labels.

Enter: clean kids' hydration.

The right drink can replace what your kids actually lose during sweat, illness, and growth spurts — without the sugar bombs, the artificial dyes, or the "natural flavors" you can't pronounce. The wrong one can hide more sugar than a soda.

I reviewed the most popular kids' hydration options on the market — the ones in every grocery store, the ones pediatricians mention, and a few clean-label newcomers — to figure out which ones I'd actually pour for my family. Some I'll keep buying. Some are going in the donate bin.

A lot of the so-called "kids' hydration" products out there are either loaded with sugar (some of them more than a Coke) or filled with artificial colors and sweeteners that pediatric organizations have been quietly warning parents about for years. Most of them haven't updated their formulas in a decade. And almost none of them deliver real electrolytes — most are flavored sugar water with a sodium garnish.

But for kids — especially kids who are running, sweating, growing, or recovering from a stomach bug — hydration needs to do real work. They need electrolytes to actually replenish what they lost, not just something sweet to chase the next pour. They need clean ingredients because their bodies are smaller and the dose-per-pound math hits harder. And they need a flavor profile they'll drink without bribery, because if they won't drink it, none of the rest matters.

So I bought a stack of the most popular options, made the kids try them blind (more on that), and rated each one based on what actually matters for a family.

I rated each based on:

- Taste (especially important when your audience has the veto power of a six-year-old)

- Electrolyte & Mineral Content (real replenishment, not sugar dressed up as electrolytes)

- Ingredients (no fillers, no Red 40, no synthetic flavors)

- Sweeteners (zero added sugar where possible, no artificial sweeteners ever)

Some surprised me. Some confirmed what I already suspected. Below, my honest rankings — so you can stop second-guessing the back of every package.
