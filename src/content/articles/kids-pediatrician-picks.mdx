The first time my pediatrician told me to stop buying Capri Sun, I felt called out. The second time she mentioned that the "fruit hydration" drink in my daughter's lunchbox had 16g of sugar per pouch, I started actually paying attention.

I have two kids — one in club soccer, one in gymnastics — and for years I'd been treating "kids' hydration" like an afterthought. Whatever was on sale at Target. Whatever the snack parent brought to the game. Whatever didn't get protested in the carpool line. And the more I read, the more obvious it became that the kids' beverage aisle was designed for parents who don't read labels.

So I put together a short list of the actual hydration drinks our pediatrician (and her office's nutritionist) had mentioned over the past two years. Then I added the most popular options I see other parents reaching for. Then I tested all of them at home — sometimes blind, sometimes not — and rated each one on what actually matters when you're pouring something for a small human.

Enter: kids' hydration that doesn't require an apology.

A real kids' hydration drink should do three things: replace what they actually lose through sweat or illness, deliver clean ingredients (because their bodies are smaller and the dose-per-pound math hits harder), and taste good enough that they'll drink it without negotiation. Most products on the shelf hit one of those three. Almost none hit all three.

I rated each based on:

- Pediatrician-approval friendliness (would the doctor in our practice actively approve, tolerate, or warn against it)

- Electrolyte & Mineral Content (real replenishment, not sugar dressed up as electrolytes)

- Ingredients (no Red 40, no artificial sweeteners, no things I can't pronounce)

- Kid-acceptance (the brutal real-world test — will my actual kids drink it twice)
